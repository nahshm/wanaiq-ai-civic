Introduction: The Evolution of Kenya's Institutional 
Framework 
The institutional architecture of the Republic of Kenya has undergone profound and continuous 
transformations since the promulgation of the 2010 Constitution. The shift from a highly 
centralized, unitary state to a decentralized, devolved system introduced multiple, intersecting 
layers of governance, fundamentally altering the distribution of political power, resource 
allocation, and public service delivery mechanisms across the nation. This structural evolution 
established a highly complex executive branch comprising national ministries and specialized 
state departments, a robust framework of independent constitutional commissions, a vast, 
historically bloated network of state corporations (parastatals), and forty-seven autonomous 
county governments. 
At the micro-administrative level, the governance structure extends downward to two hundred 
and ninety constituencies and one thousand four hundred and fifty electoral wards. This 
guarantees that the state's administrative footprint and capital development initiativesùoften 
facilitated through statutory mechanisms like the National Government Constituencies 
Development Fund (NG-CDF)ùreach the deepest grassroots levels. However, the proliferation 
of state agencies, Semi-Autonomous Government Agencies (SAGAs), and independent funds 
over the past decade has precipitated a severe fiscal and operational crisis. By early 2024, an 
assessment by the National Treasury revealed that many state corporations were struggling to 
meet their basic contractual and statutory obligations, leading to an unsustainable accumulation 
of pending bills amounting to KSh 94.4 billion. 
In response to this administrative bloat and fiscal distress, the national executive initiated 
sweeping rationalization protocols in early 2025. Culminating in Executive Order No. 1 of 2025 
and a series of high-level Cabinet directives, the government embarked on a radical 
consolidation strategy. This included the approved merger of forty-two state corporations into 
twenty streamlined entities, the outright dissolution of nine redundant agencies, the restructuring 
of six major corporations, and the divestiture of several commercial state enterprises. These 
maneuvers illustrate a broader macroeconomic imperative: aligning the state's operational 
capacity with its revenue generation capabilities while attempting to preserve the decentralized 
service delivery envisioned by the 2010 Constitution. 
This report provides an exhaustive, nuanced structural analysis and a comprehensive directory of 
all tiers of the Kenyan government. It deeply analyzes the underlying trends driving the recent

bureaucratic restructuring, the friction between devolved and centralized entities, and presents 
rigorously formatted data covering national ministries, independent commissions, state 
corporations, regional development authorities, county governments, county-level water 
corporations, and constituency-level administrative bodies. 
The National Executive: Ministries and State Departments 
The National Executive operates as the apex administrative and policy-making body of the 
Kenyan state, mandated with formulating national policy, overseeing national defense, managing 
foreign affairs, and driving macroeconomic planning. The architecture of the executive is 
inherently dynamic, frequently adjusted by the Presidency to align with the prevailing political 
economy and the specific strategic objectives of the administration, currently anchored by the 
Bottom-up Economic Transformation Agenda (BETA). 
Executive Order No. 1 of 2025 codified significant structural modifications to the national 
government, reflecting a strategic pivot toward concentrated oversight and dispersed execution. 
Notably, the Order wound up the State Department for Performance and Delivery Management, 
transferring its residual functions directly to the Executive Office of the President. 
Simultaneously, the Order established seven new, highly specialized State Departments: the 
State Department for National Government Co-ordination; Science, Research and Innovation; 
Public Investments and Assets Management; Special Programmes; Aviation and Aerospace 
Development; Children Services; and Justice, Human Rights and Constitutional Affairs. This 
restructuring reveals a second-order insight into the administration's current governance strategy. 
By centralizing performance management within the Presidency while simultaneously atomizing 
policy areas into hyper-focused State Departments, the executive is attempting to increase micro-
level policy execution efficiency while keeping a tight, centralized leash on political and 
administrative accountability. 
Furthermore, the implementation of the Government Performance Contracting Information 
System (GPCIS) underscores a paradigm shift toward data-driven bureaucratic accountability. 
The GPCIS comprises interlinked modules for negotiation, vetting, performance monitoring, 
reporting, and evaluation. All eligible Ministries, Departments, and Agencies (MDAs)ù
including Commercial and Non-Commercial State Corporations and Tertiary Institutionsùare 
now strictly mandated to utilize this system to develop and implement their FY 2025/2026 
Performance Contracts. Within this framework, specific ministries hold cross-cutting oversight 
roles. For instance, the National Treasury and Economic Planning is responsible for assessing 
annual performance for asset management and enforcing a strict 70:30 recurrent-to-development 
expenditure ratio across all MDAs, attempting to curb the voracious operational appetite of the 
state. 
The composition of the Cabinet reflects these complex mandates. While standard portfolios such 
as Defence, Health, and Education remain fixtures, newer configurations like the Ministry of 
East Africa Community, ASALs and Regional Development highlight the state's focus on 
marginalized arid lands and regional basin-based economic integration.

Chapter Fifteen Institutions: Independent Commissions and 
Offices 
Chapter 15 of the 2010 Constitution of Kenya fundamentally reimagined the relationship 
between the state apparatus and the citizenry by establishing a powerful tier of independent 
commissions and independent constitutional offices. The overarching structural objective of 
these institutions, as defined in Article 249, is to protect the sovereignty of the people, secure the 
observance of democratic values by all state organs, and ensure equitable resource distribution 
free from undue executive or political interference. 
A compelling illustration of institutional evolution and functional specialization in this space is 
the unbundling of the original Kenya National Human Rights and Equality Commission 
(KNHREC), which was initially established under Article 59. Recognizing the vast, often 
competing demands of monitoring human rights abuses, advocating for gender parity and 
minority inclusion, and ensuring administrative justice within the civil service, the legislature 
restructured KNHREC into three distinct bodies. This birthed the Kenya National Commission 
on Human Rights (KNCHR) to act as the primary state watchdog on human rights matters; the 
National Gender and Equality Commission (NGEC) to drive affirmative action and inclusion; 
and the Commission on Administrative Justice (CAJ), commonly known as the Office of the 
Ombudsman, to tackle maladministration in the public sector. 
Economically and fiscally, bodies such as the Commission on Revenue Allocation (CRA) and 
the Salaries and Remuneration Commission (SRC) play critical roles in national macroeconomic 
stability. The SRC, established under Article 230, sets and regularly reviews the remuneration 
=== PAGE 6 ===
and benefits of all State Officers while advising national and county governments on the 
compensation of all other public officers. This acts as a crucial, constitutionally mandated brake 
against runaway public sector wage bills that historically plagued the Kenyan civil service. By 
centralizing the determination of salaries, the state prevents decentralized entities and state 
corporations from unilaterally inflating executive pay scales, a practice that previously 
contributed heavily to the insolvency of many parastatals. 
Concurrently, the Office of the Auditor-General (OAG) and the Office of the Controller of 
Budget (OCOB) operate as the paramount fiscal watchdogs. The Controller of Budget authorizes 
withdrawals from public funds only if satisfied that the withdrawal is permitted by law, acting as 
an ex-ante control mechanism. The Auditor-General provides ex-post oversight, auditing the 
accounts of the national and county governments, all courts, and every state corporation. Their 
combined reports frequently highlight the systemic inefficiencies and financial misappropriations 
that eventually justify executive actions like the mass dissolution of state agencies seen in early 
2025. 
Furthermore, the Independent Electoral and Boundaries Commission (IEBC) holds the vital 
mandate of boundary delimitation, regularly reviewing the names and boundaries of 
constituencies and wards, and managing the electoral processes that populate the legislative 
branches at both the national and county levels.

State Corporations, Semi-Autonomous Agencies, 
Universities, and RDAs 
The parastatal sector in Kenya represents one of the most expansive and financially complex 
segments of the government. This tier includes Commercial State Corporations, Non-
Commercial State Corporations, Regulatory Agencies, Public Universities, and Regional 
Development Authorities (RDAs). Historically, these entities were established to provide 
strategic socio-economic interventions where private sector capital was deemed insufficient, or 
where strict regulatory oversight over crucial economic sectors was mandated. 
The State Corporations Advisory Committee (SCAC), functioning under the Presidency's Public 
Entities Oversight Services, provides the governance framework for these entities. In the 
2025/2026 fiscal cycle, SCAC set a target to evaluate the boards of three hundred and forty State 
Corporations, ensuring 100% compliance in the development and review of Human Resource 
Management instruments and the induction of board members. SCAC's guidelines dictate the 
terms of service, aligning the remuneration of chief executive officers and board chairmen with 
constitutional requirements and fiscal sustainability. 
However, over decades of political expansion, the proliferation of Semi-Autonomous 
Government Agencies (SAGAs) created vast jurisdictional overlaps, operational redundancies, 
and severe fiscal strain on the national exchequer. The breaking point was reached in early 2024 
when a comprehensive assessment by the National Treasury of two hundred and seventy-one 
state corporations (excluding those already earmarked for privatization) revealed systemic 
failures. Many corporations were defaulting on their statutory obligations, generating an 
unsustainable KSh 94.4 billion in pending bills. 
This fiscal reality catalyzed a harsh rationalization policy authorized by the Cabinet in 2025 at 
the State Lodge in Kakamega. The government recognized that many state corporations were 
performing duties that could easily be reabsorbed by their parent ministries without requiring a 
=== PAGE 9 ===
separate, expensive corporate board and management structure. Consequently, the Cabinet 
explicitly resolved to dissolve nine specific state corporations. The targeted entities for 
dissolution include the Kenya Tsetse Fly and Trypanosomiasis Eradication Council, Kenya Fish 
Marketing Authority, the Centre for Mathematics, Science and Technology Education in Africa 
(CEMASTEA), the PresidentÆs Award û Kenya, the Nuclear Power and Energy Agency, the 
Kenya National Commission for UNESCO, the Kenya Film Classification Board, the National 
Council for Nomadic Education, and the LAPSSET Corridor Development Authority. 
Beyond dissolution, a massive consolidation effort is underway, merging forty-two state 
corporations with duplicating or related mandates to form twenty streamlined entities. For 
example, higher education financing is being overhauled by merging the University Fund 
directly with the Higher Education Loans Board (HELB). Similarly, trade and tourism promotion 
are being rationalized by merging the Tourism Research Institute with the Kenya Tourism Board, 
and consolidating the Special Economic Zones Authority with the Export Processing Zones 
Authority (EPZA). Additionally, six major corporations, including the Kenya Utalii College, 
Postal Corporation of Kenya, and the National Housing Corporation, have been slated for intense 
structural restructuring. 
A parallel structural divestiture and privatization pipeline is also actively targeting high-profile 
commercial entities to inject private capital and liberalize ownership. Enterprises formally 
earmarked for privatization include the Kenyatta International Convention Centre (KICC), 
Kenya Pipeline Company (KPC), New Kenya Cooperative Creameries (KCC), National Oil 
Corporation of Kenya (NOCK), Kenya Seed Company Limited (KSC), and the Kenya Literature 
Bureau (KLB). 
Regional Development Authorities (RDAs) represent another distinct and highly critical category 
within the state corporation framework. Operating under the Ministry of East African 
Community, ASALs and Regional Development, the six RDAs utilize a basin-based approach to 
spearhead multi-purpose developmental projects that cross artificial administrative boundaries. 
These authorities include the Tana and Athi Rivers Development Authority (TARDA), Kerio 
Valley Development Authority (KVDA), Lake Basin Development Authority (LBDA), Ewaso 
Ng'iro North Development Authority (ENNDA), Ewaso Ng'iro South Development Authority 
(ENSDA), and the Coast Development Authority (CDA). Their mandate, codified in a 
comprehensive Regional Development Policy, is to implement integrated environmental 
conservation, water resource management, and regional socioeconomic development. 
Public universities form the final massive contingent of state corporations. Governed by the 
Universities Act and regulated by the Commission for University Education (CUE), Kenya has 
expanded its higher education footprint to ensure regional representation, upgrading dozens of 
constituent colleges into fully chartered public universities.

Devolved Governance: The Forty-Seven County 
Governments 
Chapter 11 of the Constitution establishes the framework for Devolution, dismantling the rigid, 
colonial-era provincial administration (which relied on centrally appointed Provincial 
Commissioners) and replacing it with forty-seven distinct County Governments. This geographic 
and political subdivision precisely mirrors the district demarcations that existed in 1992, 
elevating them to autonomous centers of political and economic power. 
Each county operates with two highly distinct arms designed to provide localized checks and 
balances: the County Executive and the County Assembly. The Executive branch is headed by a 
Governorùwho serves as the chief executive of the county for a maximum of two five-year 
termsùalongside a Deputy Governor. They govern in conjunction with an appointed County 
Executive Committee (CEC), which functions analogously to the national cabinet, holding 
portfolios tailored to local economic needs. The County Assembly, consisting of elected 
Members of County Assembly (MCAs) representing distinct localized wards, exercises 
legislative authority, passes county budgets, and maintains rigorous oversight over the executive. 
The decentralization process mandated by the Fourth Schedule of the Constitution transferred 
immense and costly service-delivery responsibilities to the counties. These functions include 
agriculture and livestock development, the management of county health facilities and 
pharmacies, pre-primary education, county transport and infrastructure development, trade 
development and regulation, physical planning, and the provision of water and sanitation 
services. 
=== PAGE 17 ===
To navigate the complex intergovernmental relations between the forty-seven distinct entities 
and the formidable national government machinery, the Council of Governors (CoG) was 
established under the Intergovernmental Relations Act. The CoG functions as the primary 
collective voice for the counties. It is instrumental in sharing developmental best practices, 
driving macro-policy coordination across regional blocs, and functioning as a statutory 
alternative dispute resolution mechanism between rival counties or between county executives 
and the national government. The CoG operates through sectoral committees chaired by the 
governors, mirroring the structure of the national Senate and National Assembly.

County-Level Corporations: Water and Sanitation 
Companies 
=== PAGE 23 ===
The transition to devolution fundamentally reorganized the provision of essential services, most 
notably the critical delivery of water and sanitation. Prior to devolution, water supply was 
fragmented across municipal councils and national boards. Under the contemporary framework 
governed by the Water Act 2016, and strictly regulated nationally by the Water Services 
Regulatory Board (WASREB), the operational mandate for water provision was transitioned into 
corporatized entities wholly owned by their respective County Governments. 
These County Water and Sewerage Companies (WSPs) function as semi-autonomous corporate 
entities. They maintain their own boards of directors and independent management structures 
while remaining accountable to the county executiveùspecifically the County Executive 
Committee Member for Waterùand ultimately to the Office of the Auditor-General (OAG) for 
their annual financial and performance audits. 
An analysis of this corporatized structural model suggests a vital, third-order economic insight 
into local public finance: transforming county water departments into independent, registered 
companies aims to legally ring-fence water revenues. By structurally insulating water tariffs and 
connection fees from the broader county treasury pool, these companies can hypothetically 
reinvest their capital directly into essential infrastructure maintenance, such as treatment 
facilities and pipeline extensions. This prevents water revenues from being cannibalized by 
unrelated, often highly inflated county recurrent expenditures, such as payroll or administrative 
travel. 
However, operational sustainability remains a persistent hurdle. WASREBÆs rigorous tariff 
conditioningùwhich segments consumers into domestic, commercial, and institutional blocks 
with escalating price thresholds based on consumption volumeùhighlights the delicate balance 
WSPs must strike. They are forced to operate as commercially viable entities while fulfilling the 
constitutional mandate of water as a fundamental human right. WASREB frequently justifies 
upward tariff reviews, such as those granted to the Nairobi City Water & Sewerage Company for 
the 2022-2025 period, specifically to ensure that the companies can expand service delivery and 
mitigate crippling levels of non-revenue water.

Micro-Level Administration: Constituencies and Wards 
At the very base of the Kenyan governance pyramid exist the two hundred and ninety 
constituencies and the one thousand four hundred and fifty wards. These granular geographic 
subdivisions serve critical dual purposes within the political and administrative framework of the 
state. Politically, they function as the direct electoral zones for Members of the National 
Assembly (MPs) and Members of the County Assembly (MCAs), respectively. Administratively, 
=== PAGE 35 ===
they act as localized, highly defined boundaries for resource deployment, demographic mapping, 
and hyper-local infrastructure development. 
The National Government Constituencies Development Fund (NG-CDF) represents the most 
critical and highly debated institutional mechanism operating at this tier. Originally established 
in 2003, and subsequently reconstituted under the NG-CDF Act of 2015 (and heavily amended in 
2016 to align with constitutional mandates), the fund exists to proactively address historic 
regional development imbalances. It achieves this by guaranteeing in law that a minimum of 2.5 
percent of the National Government's share of annual revenue is removed from the centralized 
treasury and channeled directly toward community-driven development projects in every single 
constituency across the republic. For the FY 2025/2026 budget cycle, this allocation formula 
resulted in disbursements of Ksh 221.5 million per constituency, benefiting diverse geographic 
areas such as Kinangop, Kieni, Mwea, Ruiru, Naivasha, Kanduyi, and Bobasi uniformly. 
The architectural structure of the NG-CDF generates a profound political economy dynamic that 
regularly introduces friction into intergovernmental relations. While County Governments 
(headed by powerful Governors) hold the constitutional mandate to execute the macro-
development of an area (such as building major county hospitals or paving feeder roads), the 
local Member of Parliament utilizes the NG-CDF to aggressively micro-target constituent needs, 
operating almost entirely independently of the county executive structure. MPs direct NG-CDF 
capital toward school infrastructure renovations, bursary distribution, and localized security 
installations (like police posts), thus maintaining a direct line of patronage and visible service 
delivery to their electorate. 
However, to maintain national control over these immense funds, the NG-CDF Board enforces a 
strict, centralized property ownership model. The law dictates that all fixed and movable assets 
acquired by the local Constituency Committeesùincluding office buildings, motor vehicles, and 
equipmentùare the exclusive legal property of the central NG-CDF Board in Nairobi, not the 
local community or the MP. This generates a highly agile, decentralized administrative hub in all 
290 constituencies that ultimately remains tethered to the national executive's accounting 
framework. The National Assembly actively oversees this apparatus, expediting project 
approvals and nominee vetting to ensure funds continuously flow to the grassroots. 
Beneath the constituencies lie the one thousand four hundred and fifty administrative wards. 
These wards form the basic planning unit for county-level infrastructure (managed by MCAs) 
and have increasingly become integrated into advanced national digital mapping frameworks. 
For instance, humanitarian and governmental datasets now utilize unified identifiers compatible 
with the District Health Information Software (DHIS2) platform at the ward level. This 
standardizes the geographic topology across the nation, aiding in rapid humanitarian 
interventions, accurate healthcare facility mapping, and granular resource allocation during 
crises, shifting the ward from a purely political boundary into an instrumental data node for state 
planning.

Conclusion and Strategic Outlook 
The institutional matrix of the Kenyan state reflects a continuous, often tumultuous search for 
equilibrium between centralized administrative efficiency and the constitutional mandate for 
devolved democratic participation. The exhaustive directories provided throughout this structural 
analysis map a state that is deeply and intentionally layered. From the macro-level machinations 
of the Executive Office of the President and the fiscal watchdogs within the independent 
constitutional commissions, down through the corporatized water service providers in the forty-
seven counties, and ultimately to the NG-CDF committees operating at the highly granular 
constituency level, the bureaucratic footprint of the state is vast and inescapable. 
However, the prevailing administrative narrative moving into 2026 is one defined by severe 
fiscal austerity, institutional reckoning, and aggressive organizational consolidation. The state's 
unprecedented maneuvers to forcefully merge forty-two Semi-Autonomous Government 
Agencies and completely dissolve nine others signal a critical realization at the highest levels of 
the executive: the bureaucratic fragmentation that characterized the last two decades is 
structurally and fiscally unsustainable. The accumulation of KSh 94.4 billion in pending bills by 
these peripheral entities forced the hand of the state, shifting the governance paradigm from 
endless expansion to rigid rationalization. 
By aggressively mandating performance contracting across all tiers via the GPCIS platform , 
rationalizing public funds into coherent entities, and returning orphaned state corporations to the 
direct oversight of their core parent ministries , the Kenyan government is attempting to 
streamline public service delivery without violating the core tenets of the 2010 Constitution. The 
ultimate success of this massive rationalization initiative will depend heavily on the continued 
vigilance and uncompromised autonomy of the Chapter 15 institutions. Specifically, the Office 
of the Auditor-General and the Salaries and Remuneration Commission must ensure that newly 
merged super-entities do not simply inherit the massive liabilities and inflated payrolls of their 
predecessors. If executed with precision, this structural overhaul holds the potential to transition 
Kenya's public sector from a historically bloated administrative apparatus into a lean, data-driven 
engine capable of facilitating sustained economic transformation.
